The 4 Gaps This Plan Targets
Every session in this plan traces back to one of these four areas.
1 · Getting Down Quickly — Wider stance, collapse dive mechanics, bottom-hand technique. Your 5'7" frame is actually a biomechanical advantage for low dives.
2 · Extension Range — Power step dive mechanics + lateral plyometric power. Positioning intelligence compensates for height on shots beyond reach.
3 · 1v1 Situations — Distance-based decision framework (passive set / close down / spread / smother). The most common youth GK mistake is staying back — train the decision first, then the technique.
4 · Goal Kick Technique — Punt and goal kick are biomechanically different. Ankle lock, plant foot, approach angle — trained in isolation before integrating. Weekend sessions only.

🧤 FIFA 11+ Shoulder Warm-Up · 5 min Every pre-practice session, every time
✅ This warm-up opens every supplemental session. Never skip it. I, Y, T positions activate rotator cuff; push-up variations prime the shoulder for landing load.
I-Position Raise Duration10 reps
WHAT = Stand, arms at sides. Raise both arms straight in front to shoulder height (thumbs up), hold 1 second at top, lower slowly. This is the I-shape.
💡 Squeeze your shoulder blades together slightly as you raise. This warms up the posterior rotator cuff — exactly what takes the load when you dive and land on one arm.
Y-Position Raise Duration10 reps
WHAT = Same starting position. Raise arms to a Y shape — 45 degrees out from straight ahead, thumbs up, to shoulder height. Hold 1 sec, lower slowly.
💡 Keep your chest up — don't let your upper back round forward. The Y hits the lower trapezius, which stabilizes your shoulder during full-extension dives.
T-Position Raise Duration10 reps
WHAT = Raise arms straight out to the sides — pure T shape, thumbs up, to shoulder height. Hold 1 sec, lower slowly.
💡 Slight external rotation (thumbs up, not down) is the key. Thumbs-down internally rotates the shoulder and reduces subacromial space — that's how you get impingement over a season.
Push-Up with Plus Duration8 reps
WHAT = Standard push-up. At the top of each rep, push your shoulder blades apart an extra inch — "plus" them forward. Pause 1 sec, then lower.
💡 The "plus" at the top is the serratus anterior firing. That muscle is a GK's shoulder armor — it keeps your scapula against your ribs during impact. Don't skip the plus.
Diagonal Arm Pattern Duration8 each side
WHAT = Standing. Sweep one arm from low across your body (near opposite hip) diagonally up to high reach on the same side — like drawing a sword and raising it overhead. Slow and controlled. 8 reps each arm.
💡 This mimics the full-extension dive reach pattern. Feel the stretch across your chest and lat at the top. Move with control — this is prep, not speed.
👟 Footwork & Set Position · 5 min Wider stance + shuffle precision
📐 Stance width target: ~25 in (roughly 75% of your leg length). This is significantly wider than shoulder-width. A single session of drilling this has been shown to add ~17 in of goal coverage on low saves. It will feel awkward at first — that's correct.
Wide Stance Drill Duration2 min
WHAT = Place two cones approximately 25 in apart (use your shoe as a rough ruler — about 2.5 shoe-lengths). Set your feet just outside the cones in your GK ready position. Hold the stance for 3 seconds while actively feeling your weight distribution. Shuffle 3 steps to the right, stop, reset feet at the correct wide width. Shuffle 3 steps back left, reset. Continue for 2 min.
💡 Your instinct will be to narrow your stance when you shuffle — fight it. Every time you stop, check: are your feet still outside the cones? Weight should be on the balls of your feet, knees bent, shoulders over toes. If your feet are inside the cones, widen them before the next shuffle.
Shuffle-Set-Shuffle Duration2 sets × 6 reps
WHAT = Set 3 cones in a line, ~2 yards apart. Start at the left cone. Shuffle right to middle cone — stop and achieve a full correct set position (feet wide, weight forward, hands up). Then shuffle right to the far cone — set position. Shuffle back left to middle — set. Shuffle all the way to start — set. That's 1 rep. 6 reps each set.
💡 The "set" is not a pause — it's an active ready state. Hips low, hands between waist and chest, eyes forward. You're not resting between shuffles; you're arriving in the position you'd be in when a shot is released. The stop should be sharp, not a drift.
⚡ GK Plyometrics · 8 min Lateral power — most GK-specific plyo category
📊 Volume: Week 1–3 = 60–80 total foot contacts · Week 4–6 = 100–120 contacts · Week 7–8 = drop 1 set per exercise · 48 hrs minimum between plyo sessions (Mon → Wed ✓) · Land softly — ground contact quality matters more than height
Lateral Bounds / Skater Jumps Sets3 Reps5 each side Rest30s
WHAT = Push off hard off one leg to the side, land on the opposite single leg, stick the landing for 1 full second before pushing back. Like a speed skater. Cover as much lateral ground as possible each bound.
💡 The "stick" landing is non-negotiable — it trains the deceleration mechanics that control your dive. If you can't stick it (wobble, step out), reduce the distance. 1-second freeze = one rep done right. This is the single most GK-specific plyometric exercise in the plan.
Pogo Jumps / Ankle Bounces Sets2 Reps10 Rest20s
WHAT = Both feet together, jump straight up using only your ankle/calf — minimal knee bend, minimal ground contact time. Like bouncing on a pogo stick. 10 consecutive reps as fast as possible off the ground.
💡 Ground contact should feel like touching a hot stove — quick and light. If you're bending your knees significantly, you've slowed it down. This trains ankle stiffness and the elastic energy return you need for a split step.
Countermovement Jumps (CMJ) Sets3 Reps4 Rest30s
WHAT = Stand tall. Quickly drop into a quarter squat (dip phase), then immediately explode upward as high as possible — arms drive up to help. Land softly, feet shoulder-width, absorb through ankles/knees/hips. Full stop before next rep.
💡 The dip should be fast — it's a trigger, not a slow squat. The faster the dip-to-jump transition, the more elastic energy you use. Max height every rep. Soft landing every rep. This is your vertical leap for high-ball claims.
Single-Leg Lateral Hops Sets2 Reps4 each side Rest20s
WHAT = Balance on one foot beside a cone or a crack/line on the ground. Hop laterally over it and land on the same foot. Immediately hop back. That's 1 rep. 4 reps = 4 round trips. Keep it quick but controlled.
💡 Land with a slight knee bend — "soft landing" is the goal. This is unilateral lateral power, directly training the push-off leg for collapse dives. If you're losing balance on landing, slow down. Balance before speed.
🤸 Collapse Dive Progression · 7 min Ball contacts ground first — always
🌿 You need ~3 yards of soft grass width for diving reps. Grass perimeter or the edge of the field sideline works. If the turf is rock-hard or the area is too narrow, do the kneeling-only version and skip standing collapse today.
Kneeling Collapse Dive Duration8 reps each side
WHAT = Start on both knees, upright. Place a ball 1 yard to your right side. Fall sideways to your right — reach the bottom hand (right hand) behind and slightly under the ball, top hand (left hand) seals on top. Ball is the FIRST thing to contact the ground. Recover, reset on knees. 8 reps each side. Roll the ball back each time.
💡 This drill is about hand positioning and ball contact sequence — not speed. Bottom hand gets behind the ball like you're scooping it. Top hand closes on top. If your hip or elbow hits the ground before the ball, that's the mistake. Reset and think "ball first" before each rep.
Crouching Collapse Dive Duration8 reps each side
WHAT = Start in a deep squat/crouch position. Ball placed 1 yard to the side. Same technique — fall sideways, sweep the near-side leg out, bottom hand to ball, top hand seals. More lateral distance than the kneeling version.
💡 The near-side leg sweep is the new element here. Sweep it out (not back), which transfers your weight horizontally. Don't try to stand first — you're collapsing from the crouch, not diving from standing. If you feel yourself sitting back, your hips are too high.
Standing Collapse Dive Duration8 reps each side
WHAT = Full GK ready position (wide stance, knees bent). Self-roll the ball gently 1–1.5 yards to one side. Execute the collapse dive: sweep near leg, lateral weight transfer, bottom hand behind ball, top hand seals. Ball contacts ground first. Return to set position between reps.
💡 Self-rolling the ball teaches you to react to a moving target, not a stationary one. Roll it gently — this is technique training, not reaction speed training. Check three things every rep: (1) Did ball hit ground before your hip? (2) Is bottom hand BEHIND the ball (not on top)? (3) Did you maintain your wide stance in the set position before the dive?
🛡️ Injury Prevention Prehab · 5 min Wrists + hips — close every pre-practice session
Wrist Circles + Finger Extensions Duration1 min
WHAT = 10 slow wrist circles each direction, then splay your fingers wide and hold 3 seconds, then make a fist and hold 3 seconds. Repeat 5 times. Finish with 10 ball squeezes if you have a ball handy (squeeze and hold 3 sec each).
💡 GK wrists and fingers take impact every time you dive. This 60-second prehab routine is small protection against cumulative overload. If your wrists or fingers are sore from the previous session, this is also a red flag to mention to your coach.
90/90 Hip Switches Duration8 reps
WHAT = Sit on the ground with both legs in a 90/90 position (front leg bent 90° forward, back leg bent 90° to the side). Slowly rotate your hips to switch — front leg goes back, back leg comes forward. That's 1 rep. Slow and controlled, hands light on the ground for support.
💡 Hip flexor tightness is the #1 groin/hip injury risk for GKs who dive frequently — the lateral weight transfer loads the hip hard. These switches open that range in both directions. If one side feels much tighter, that's the side to spend more time on.
Lateral Leg Swings Duration10 each side
WHAT = Stand on one leg, hold a cone or wall for balance. Swing the free leg out to the side and across your body — gradually increasing range each swing. 10 swings, then switch legs. Keep the movement flowing and relaxed.
💡 Let gravity do the work — don't force the range. The adductors (inner thigh) take eccentric load every time you dive, especially the wide-stance collapse. Loose, warm adductors = safer diving and better lateral range.
Hip Flexor Stretch Duration30 sec each side
WHAT = Lunge forward on one leg, back knee on the ground. Shift your hips forward until you feel a stretch in the front of your back hip. Hold 30 seconds, then switch. Optional: add a slight rotation toward the front knee to increase the stretch.
💡 End every pre-practice session with this. You're about to go into team practice and a 90-minute game load. Tight hip flexors going into team practice = reduced sprint mechanics, reduced push-off range. This is prep, not just stretching.

👟 Footwork & Set Position · 5 min Footwork patterns + split step timing
Diamond Footwork Duration2 sets
WHAT = Place 4 cones in a diamond shape, ~3 yards between each point (fits in ~5-yard space when compressed slightly). Start at the bottom cone. Shuffle laterally to right cone, set position. Shuffle forward-diagonally to top cone, set. Shuffle laterally to left cone, set. Shuffle back to start, set. That's 1 rep. 4 reps each set. Then reverse direction.
💡 The point of this drill is the set position at each cone — not the shuffle speed. Every stop should be crisp: feet wide, weight forward, hips down. You're training the GK shuffle pattern that covers the arc of your post-to-post movement.
Split Step Timing Drill Duration10 reps
WHAT = Set a ball on the ground 2 yards to your right side. Stand in set position facing forward. Use your phone timer on a 10-second countdown (or count "1-2-NOW" aloud). The moment you hear the beep or say "NOW" — execute a split step (small jump, land in wide stance) and immediately dive to the ball, using your collapse technique. Reset and repeat 10 times, alternating which side the ball is on.
💡 The split step should happen slightly BEFORE "NOW" — you initiate the jump based on the anticipated signal, not in reaction to it. Research shows dive speed is 3-4% faster when the split step initiates during knee flexion of the forward motion. The split loads your legs; the dive fires from that loaded position. If you're flat-footed when the signal comes, you're already late.
🙌 Ball Handling · 5 min Hand shape + reaction + catching mechanics
✅ All ball handling fits in a standing footprint. You only need the ball and yourself.
W-Shape Hand Drill Duration15 catches
WHAT = Hold the ball at arm's length, then self-toss it to chest height — not too hard. Catch it with a W-hand shape: thumbs pointing toward each other behind the ball, fingers spread wide on the side of the ball, palms not touching. Catch, reset, toss again. 15 catches.
💡 After each catch, look at your hands before resetting — did your thumbs end up pointing together or apart? Apart = wrong (ball goes through). Together = W-shape = correct. Soft hands absorb the ball; stiff hands deflect it. This is muscle memory work, not a conditioning drill.
Quick Hands Ground Pick-Up Duration10 each side
WHAT = Place a ball on the ground 1 yard to your right. From set position, step laterally, drop down into a low scoop: right hand behind the ball, left hand on top — "baseball scoop" grip. Pick it up, bring it to your chest, return to set position. That's 1 rep. 10 reps each side.
💡 The hand behind the ball (bottom hand) is the primary save hand — it stops the ball from going past. The top hand is the safety. The sequence is always: bottom hand makes contact first, top hand seals. A ball that squirts under your bottom hand means your hand was on TOP of the ball, not behind it.
Reaction Catch Duration10 reps each variation
WHAT = Variation 1 — Hold ball at arm's length in front of you, release it, catch before second bounce. Variation 2 — Hold ball behind your back over one shoulder, release it, spin and catch before second bounce. Alternate between variations, 10 reps each. Drop to your dominant hand side first, then non-dominant.
💡 This drill is about reaction time — catching before the second bounce forces a genuine quick response, not a slow reach-down. Variation 2 trains your peripheral awareness and your spin-to-the-ground recovery after facing forward. This simulates a deflected shot rolling behind you.
High Ball Claim — Vertical Duration10 reps
WHAT = Self-toss ball above your head. Jump off two feet, catch the ball at your highest point with W-hand shape, bring it to your chest, land on both feet. That's 1 rep. 10 reps. This is a stationary vertical claim — no approach run needed, fits in confined space.
💡 Catch at the TOP of your jump — not on the way up, not on the way down. At the top, you have a brief moment of near-zero velocity. Catching then means you can absorb the ball cleanly. Catching on the way down adds momentum that drives the ball into your chest awkwardly.

🤸 1v1 Body Shape Practice · 5 min K-block · Spread · Smother — decision BEFORE technique
📏 Distance-based 1v1 framework. Research on 323 EPL + World Cup 1v1s found 4 distinct techniques. The most common youth GK strategy (staying back and reacting) is also the LEAST effective. You have ~300–320ms of decision time — enough to commit early if you've trained the decision.
1v1 Decision Framework
Beyond 8 ydsPassive Set — standard ready position, read the shot
Ball in transitAggressive Set — close down quickly while ball is moving or attacker's touch is loose. Set feet the moment they regain control
4–7 yds, centralK-Block / Spread — body as wide as possible. 10% more likely to save than passive set at this range
Loose ballSmother — very low, diving at the feet. Only for genuinely loose balls
⚠️ Watch the kicking foot specifically — not the ball, not the hips. The kicking foot angle tells you shot direction 40–60ms before the ball leaves the foot.
Body Shape Station Drill Duration3 reps × 3 positions
WHAT = Set 2 cones 3 yards apart as a practice "goal." Practice each of the three positions at two distances: 5 yards from the cones and 3 yards from the cones. K-Block: drop to one knee with arms wide, filling vertical space. Spread: stand with feet very wide, arms out to sides, lean slightly forward. Smother: very low, body spread flat toward the ball. Hold each position for 2 full seconds. 3 reps of each position at each distance.
💡 This is stationary positioning practice — not a reaction drill. The point is to feel each position in your body, so that when you need it in a live situation, your muscles already know the shape. Two seconds at each position means you're actually HOLDING the correct shape, not just approximating it.
Distance Call Drill Duration10 reps
WHAT = Stand in set position. Starting from 15 yards back (use cones or a mental marker), walk slowly toward your 2-cone "goal." As you walk in, call out the technique you'd use based on your current distance: "SET" when you'd be in passive set (beyond 8 yards), "CLOSING" when you'd be actively closing down (8–5 yards), "SPREAD" when you're in close central range (under 5 yards). Do this 10 times at different starting approaches.
💡 The verbalization matters — saying "CLOSING" out loud forces you to commit to the decision, which is the whole problem most youth GKs have in live 1v1s. You KNOW what to do, but you delay the decision. This drill trains the decision reflex, not the physical movement.
🛡️ Core + Prehab · 5 min Stability base for diving mechanics
Plank Hold Duration2 × 30 sec
WHAT = Forearm plank — elbows under shoulders, body in a straight line from head to heels. Hold 30 seconds. Rest 15 sec. Repeat. Do NOT let hips sag or pike up.
💡 Your core is the transfer mechanism between your legs (push-off) and your arms (catch). A weak core loses energy in that chain — your dive feels sluggish even if your legs are fast. Squeeze your glutes and abs simultaneously. If your hips are sagging, drop your knees instead of letting form break.
Side Plank — Hip Abductor Hold Duration20 sec each side
WHAT = Side plank position — one elbow on the ground, feet stacked. Lift your hips off the ground, hold 20 seconds. Hips should be in a straight line — not sagging, not piked. Switch sides.
💡 The side plank specifically loads your hip abductors — the gluteus medius and minimus. These are the muscles that control your landing stability on single-leg collapse dives. Weak hip abductors = wobbly landings. This is two minutes of direct hip stability training.
90/90 Hip Switches + Hip Flexor Stretch Duration5 min total
WHAT = 8 reps of 90/90 hip switches: sit on the ground with both legs in a 90/90 position (front leg bent 90° forward, back leg bent 90° to the side). Slowly rotate your hips to switch — front leg goes back, back leg comes forward. That's 1 rep. Slow and controlled, hands light on the ground for support. Then: hip flexor lunge stretch — kneel forward on one leg, back knee on the ground. Shift your hips forward until you feel a stretch in the front of your back hip. Hold 30 seconds. Switch sides. Optional: add a slight rotation toward the front knee to deepen the stretch.
💡 Close every pre-practice session the same way. The hip mobility work before team practice has a direct carryover to your sprint and change-of-direction quality in the session that follows. It's not just prehab — it's preparation.

🥅 Block 1 — Goal Kick Technique · 15 min Open field · 15–20 kicks per session · Quality over quantity
⚠️ Punt ≠ Goal kick. Your punt is already strong (height, distance, placement). Your goal kick lacks all three. This is almost certainly a technique-transfer problem — specifically: ankle lock, plant foot placement, and approach angle. Train only ONE element per week for the first 3 weeks. Integration comes later.
Goal Kick Technique — Key Elements
Approach Angle30–45° from kick direction. Elite GKs average ~43.8°. Too straight-on = no hip rotation. Too wide = off-balance contact.
Plant Foot~2–4 in beside the ball, level with ball, toes pointing at target. Too far back = scoop (thin contact). Too close = foot collision.
Strike PointInstep (laces/metatarsal area) contacts JUST BELOW the ball's midline — NOT underneath. Underneath = high ball, no distance.
Ankle LockFirmly plantarflexed (toe pointed) and RIGID through impact. Most likely failure point since punting is more forgiving of a loose ankle.
Follow-ThroughLand several steps ahead of where the ball sat. Full proximal-to-distal chain: pelvis → hip → thigh → shank → foot.
📅 Weekly progression: Week 1 = ankle lock only · Week 2 = plant foot only · Week 3 = approach angle only · Week 4+ = integration. Expected improvement: Weeks 1–3 cleaner contact, Weeks 4–6 noticeable height/accuracy, Week 8+ measurable distance gains of 5–10 yards.
Ankle Lock Isolation Confined OK Duration15 reps · Week 1
WHAT = Hold ball at waist height. Lock your ankle hard (toe pointed, rigid — like you're wearing a cast). Drop the ball onto your instep. If ankle is locked correctly, ball bounces cleanly upward 2–3 feet. If ball squirts sideways or goes at an angle, ankle was loose. 15 reps. This is the one goal kick drill that can be done in confined space if needed.
💡 "Rigid as a board" — before each drop, consciously clench your ankle into the locked position. The loose ankle is the #1 reason your goal kicks lack height and distance. A locked ankle transfers the full kinetic chain. A loose ankle absorbs energy and deflects the ball sideways. If the ball bounces back straight up: you did it right.
Plant Foot Placement Drill Duration10 reps · Week 2
WHAT = Place a small marker (coin, piece of tape) where your plant foot should land: ~2–4 in to the side of the ball, roughly level with it. Full approach run to the ball — but DO NOT kick. Just practice hitting the plant foot marker perfectly. No kick, just the approach and plant. 10 reps. Then add the kick for 5 more reps.
💡 Plant foot too far back = you scoop underneath the ball (it pops up short). Plant foot level or slightly ahead = you strike the equatorial midline and drive through. The marker forces you to be precise. After 10 no-kick reps, your plant foot should land on that spot automatically.
Short-Distance Goal Kicks — 15–20 yards Duration10–15 kicks
WHAT = From a standing start or short approach, kick the ball to a target cone at 15–20 yards. Focus on clean contact and getting the ball airborne with the correct low-to-high trajectory. Don't measure distance — measure trajectory. Ball should leave the ground at a 40–50° angle, not near-vertical and not flat.
💡 A flat kick (ball barely leaves the ground) = striking too high on the ball. A near-vertical kick (ball pops up straight) = striking too low (underneath). Target the midline-minus zone. Once you're getting clean contact at 15 yards with good trajectory, add 5 yards.
Progressive Distance Goal Kicks Duration10–15 kicks · Week 4+
WHAT = When at a park with a regulation goal: full goal kicks from the 6-yard box to midfield-distance targets. Use cones or existing field markings. Add 5 yards of distance target each week once technique is clean at shorter range. 10–15 kicks maximum per session — quality over volume.
💡 Don't add distance until the short kicks are consistently airborne with good trajectory. More force applied to a bad technique just makes a worse kick go further wrong. Earn the distance with clean mechanics first.
📤 Block 2 — Distribution Practice · 10 min Throws + driven passes + punt maintenance
✅ Your punts are excellent — don't over-train them. This block is maintenance for punting and development for throws and driven passes. 10 minutes, well-placed targets, track accuracy mentally.
Overarm Throw Accuracy Duration15 throws
WHAT = Place 3 cones at 15, 20, and 25 yards. 5 throws to each cone target. Overarm throw (like a quarterback) — step into the throw with your opposite foot, arm follows through toward target. Track how many hit within 2 yards of each cone.
💡 Throw accuracy matters at ECNL level — a misdirected throw to feet instead of into stride creates unnecessary pressure. Step into it. Wrist snap at release, fingers rolling over the top of the ball. If you're consistently missing left or right, check your release point (too early = push left, too late = pull right for right-handed throwers).
Driven Pass to Target Duration10 each foot
WHAT = Ground-rolling driven passes to a cone at 20–30 yards. Focus on weight and accuracy — the ball should reach the cone with good pace, not dribbling to a stop 5 yards short. 10 with dominant foot, 10 with non-dominant.
💡 GKs are increasingly expected to play out from the back at ECNL level. A driven pass that's accurate AND well-weighted bypasses the press and puts teammates into space. Non-dominant foot distribution is a visible differentiator in tryouts — coaches notice when a GK only goes one way.
Punt Maintenance Duration10 punts
WHAT = 10 punts to a target zone at 35–40 yards. Focus on landing placement — inside a 5-yard zone, not just distance. Note: this is the last block of the distribution section, not the first. Your punts are already good. Maintain what's working.
💡 Don't change your punt technique. If it's working — height, distance, placement — don't tinker with it. These 10 punts are a touch/rhythm session. The only thing to focus on is placement accuracy to a specific zone, since that's the ECNL-level expectation.

🟠 Partner Block — Dad Available · 20–25 min Requires a server for all exercises in this block
🤝 All exercises in this block require an active server. Dad serves the ball — Eli reads, reacts, and executes. If Dad leaves early, switch to the Solo Block for remaining exercises.
Extension Dive — Served Balls Duration6 each side
WHAT = Eli stands in set position. Dad serves (rolls or underarm throws) the ball 1.5–2.5 yards beyond Eli's comfortable collapse range — to the side, at varying heights from ground to waist. Eli executes a power-step dive: load the near foot, explosive push-off to become airborne, reach and catch or deflect. 6 reps each side. Dad varies placement each rep.
💡 The power step starts from the near foot — it's a sideways explosive push, not a step then jump. Your contralateral leg (opposite to dive direction) provides dominant drive. After the dive, don't rush up — reset fully, check your set position width, then signal Dad for the next serve.
Reaction Saves — Low + Mid Height Duration10 serves total
WHAT = Eli faces the goal (or a target cone). Dad stands 8–10 yards away with multiple balls. Eli assumes set position. Dad drives or rolls a ball at any angle — low corners, mid-height to sides. Eli reads and executes (collapse dive or power step based on distance). No pattern — Dad varies placement deliberately. 10 serves.
💡 Watch Dad's shoulder and arm position — not his eyes, not his body. The serve angle tells you direction 50ms before the ball is released, just like a kicking foot tells you shot direction before contact. React to the cue, not the ball. This is the closest approximation to in-game shot-stopping you can get without a goal.
1v1 Decision Walk-Through Duration10 reps
WHAT = Phase 1 (Weeks 1–3) — Distance recognition only. Dad dribbles slowly from 15 yards in. Eli calls the technique: "SET" (beyond 8 yds), "CLOSING" (8–5 yds), "SPREAD" (under 5 central). No diving — just verbalized decision practice. Phase 2+ (Weeks 4+) — Live 1v1. Dad receives ball at 12 yards, takes a touch, Eli reads and executes appropriate technique. Full speed. 6–8 reps.
💡 Phase 1: say the technique out loud, every time. Phase 2+: your closing window is when the ball is in transit — that's when you move. The moment Dad's foot makes contact with the ball for the touch, you close. The moment he regains control, you set. Don't commit BEFORE he touches it. Don't wait until he's shooting to decide.
High Ball Claim — With Server Duration6 reps
WHAT = Dad throws the ball high into the air (lofted, 10–15 yards above ground at peak). Eli takes a 2–3 step approach, times the jump, catches the ball at the highest point with W-hand shape, brings to chest, lands on two feet. 6 reps. Vary the serve height and direction slightly each rep.
💡 Timing is everything — the approach steps set your jump timing. Two aggressive steps, then explosive jump. Catch it at the TOP of your arc. Call "KEEPER" on every rep — this is a habit to build. In a game, claiming a high ball without calling it is a collision waiting to happen.

WHAT = Start kneeling, place ball 1.5 yards to your side — just beyond collapse range from your knees. Load your near-side leg, explosively push laterally to reach and save the ball. This is the foundational movement for the full power step dive. 6 reps each side.
💡 From your knees, the near-side leg load is more isolated — you can really feel the drive mechanism. This is a good technical rehearsal for the movement pattern before standing power step dives. Focus on the explosive push quality, not how far you get.

8-Week Periodization

Each phase builds on the previous. See the "Coach Focus" note each week — bring that specific ask to Thursday GK training. The plan gives you the repetition; your coach gives you the technical feedback you can't get solo.

Phase 1 — Foundation Weeks 1–3

Build the movement patterns correctly before adding intensity. Week 1 is deliberately light — establish baselines, not PRs.

Pre-Practice Week 1 = 20 min only. Progress to 25 min by Week 2, 30 min by Week 3. Low-to-moderate intensity (RPE 5–6). Wide stance drill and collapse dive mechanics are the priority — get these right now before layering on difficulty.
Plyometrics 60–80 total foot contacts across Monday + Wednesday pre-practice (combined). Stick landings 1 second. Do NOT add tuck jumps yet. Foundation volume only.
Weekend Goal kick isolation: Week 1 = ankle lock drill only. Week 2 = plant foot placement drill only. Week 3 = approach angle. No integration yet. 15 kicks per session. Distribution maintenance as written.
1v1 Work Body shape practice only (pre-practice Wed). Distance call verbalization drills. No live 1v1 scenarios yet — learn the framework first.
Baseline Test At end of Week 1 (weekend session): measure lateral dive reach from standing, standing broad jump, T-test time. Record in Goals tab. This is a baseline, not a performance — go at 70%.
Coach Focus "I'm working on wider set position and collapse dive mechanics this week — can we reinforce that in our session? Specifically the near-side leg sweep and bottom-hand positioning."
Week 1 pre-practice is 20 minutes, intentionally. The goal is a successful first week, not a hard week. Build the habit of arriving early before building the intensity of the session.
Phase 2 — Development Weeks 4–6

Push intensity and add complexity to movement patterns. Live 1v1 work starts. Extension diving with placement variation. Peak training load in Weeks 5–6.

Pre-Practice Full 25–30 min. RPE 7–8. Add reaction elements to footwork: self-cued direction changes (close eyes briefly, open and shuffle to a random cone), random cone touches. Wednesday: add tuck jumps to plyometric block.
Plyometrics Progress to 100–120 total foot contacts across Mon + Wed. Add tuck jumps on Wednesday (2 × 4). Maintain stick landing quality — if stick landings are getting sloppy, reduce volume before increasing it.
Weekend Goal kicks: Week 4 = integrate ankle lock + plant foot together. Week 5 = add approach angle. Week 6 = full technique integration at progressive distances (add 5 yards). Extension diving: increase placement variation and speed of serve.
1v1 Work Live 1v1 scenarios with Dad start in Week 4 (partner weekend only). Eli closes when ball is in transit, sets when Dad regains control, executes framework. 6–8 live reps. Phase 2 is when this becomes game-realistic.
Mid-Point Test At end of Week 4 (weekend session): re-test lateral dive reach, standing broad jump, T-test. Compare to baseline. You should see noticeable improvements in lateral reach by now from the plyometric adaptation.
Coach Focus "I'm working on 1v1 decision-making — can we include breakaway scenarios this week? Specifically practicing the close-down timing and which technique to use at different distances."
Weeks 5–6 are your peak training weeks. You may feel tired on Thursday double-session days — that's expected. If Wednesday pre-practice is affecting Thursday quality, drop Wednesday to mobility-only that week. Thursday GK coaching is higher priority than Wednesday supplemental.
Phase 3 — Sharpening Weeks 7–8

Maintain intensity, reduce volume. Trust the work. Build confidence, not fitness — the fitness is already built.

Pre-Practice Reduce to 20 min. Cut 1 set from each plyometric exercise. Maintain RPE 8 intensity on the reps that remain. Full warm-up and prehab still included — don't cut corners on the bookends.
Plyometrics Monday only (drop Wednesday plyometrics entirely). Maintain intensity, 1 set per exercise only. Focus on quality over quantity — this is your last heavy plyometric week.
Weekend Simulate tryout demands: distribution circuit under time pressure, goal kicks to tight accuracy targets (2-yard zone), confidence-building drills with high success rate. High-success-rate reps, not maximum difficulty.
Final Test At end of Week 7: re-test lateral dive reach, broad jump, T-test. This is your final progress measure before the taper. The numbers should reflect 6 weeks of adaptation.
Coach Focus "Tryouts are in 2 weeks — can we simulate tryout-format drills? Specifically short high-volume shot-stopping, a 1v1 breakaway rep, and a few high-ball claims under pressure."
NO new exercises or techniques in Weeks 7 or 8. If something isn't working, your job is not to fix it in the last 2 weeks — your job is to trust the patterns you've built and execute what you know.
Phase 4 — Taper Final 7–10 days before tryout

100% frequency, ~90% intensity, ~50% volume. Feel sharp, not tired. The work is done — this phase is about arriving ready, not adding more.

Pre-Practice 15 min only — FIFA 11+ Shoulder warm-up + footwork + light plyometrics (pogo jumps + 2 sets of lateral bounds only). No diving in pre-practice during taper.
Plyometrics Last plyo session: 7–10 days before tryout (coordinate with Thursday GK coach). After that, no plyometrics at all. This is not optional — plyometric fatigue accumulates and peaks 48–72 hours post-session.
Weekend Weekend before tryout: 20–30 min maximum. Goal kicks (10 reps, feel good) + light distribution only. No extension diving, no 1v1 intensity work. End the session feeling sharp, not depleted.
Final 48 hrs Light ball handling only — W-hand catches, reaction catches, a few self-serve high balls. Visualization. Mobility. Sleep. No new stimulus.
Coach Focus "Tryout is this week. Can we keep tonight's session clean and confidence-focused? I want to finish feeling good, not gassed." — Coordinate with GK coach to time the last heavy session 5–7 days before tryout.
The training is done. The adaptation happened in Weeks 3–6. The taper is how you make sure all that adaptation is available on tryout day. Showing up tired to a tryout wastes 8 weeks of work. Trust it.

Height & Positioning Note

At 5'7", you're below average for GK at ECNL level — but you're 15 and still growing. The plan already accounts for this:

🟢 Biomechanical advantage: Lower center of gravity accelerates lateral dive mechanics. The physics actually favor you on low saves.
🔵 Highest-leverage compensatory skill: Positioning intelligence. Come 1–2 yards off the line for close-range threats to narrow the visible target. Stay within 1–2 steps of the goal line for long-range threats. A well-positioned shorter GK covers more net than a poorly-positioned taller one.
🟣 Ask your Thursday coach: Positioning drills — specifically where to set your starting position based on ball location and distance. This is the highest-leverage compensatory skill you can develop in the next 8 weeks.
🚨 Load monitoring — watch for these signals: Persistent shoulder or wrist soreness after diving sessions · Reduced willingness to dive fully (body protecting itself) · Hip or groin tightness that doesn't resolve after warm-up · Noticeably lower jump height or sluggish movement in pre-practice · Low energy on Thursday double-session days. If 2 or more of these appear in the same week → drop Wednesday pre-practice to 10-min mobility only and reassess.
